Singapore as a Travel Destination

Section 01
Methodology
How this analysis was conducted, and what each data source contributes

This report draws on two layers of Meltwater GenAI Lens data, both covering the 7 June – 6 July 2026 window.

Layer 1 — prompt-level responses. Two prompts — "best places to visit in singapore" and "singapore for holiday" (folder: Tourism) — were run across 5 AI models (ChatGPT/GPT-5, Deepseek, Google AI Mode, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity/Sonar), producing 125 total responses (58 on "Visit Singapore," 67 on "Singapore as a destination"). Each response was enriched with sentiment, key phrases, named organisations/products, and cited links. This layer powers the perception, sentiment and prompt-level sections.

Layer 2 — broader entity tracking. A separate, wider entity-tracking view spanning the same Tourism folder supplies: a 10-entity visibility heatmap across 5 models, a 40-entity mentions/visibility/prevalence trend table with 15 bi-weekly snapshots, a ranked list of the top 100 cited sources, and a ranked list of the top 7 cited travel journalists/bylines. This layer is broader than the 2 sampled prompts and powers the narrative-ownership, entity-scorecard and channel sections.

No single brand is tracked as "primary" in either layer (every entity is flagged isPrimaryBrand: null/false) — this is a destination-level perception and narrative-ownership study, not a single-client visibility audit. Findings should be read as "who is currently telling Singapore's tourism story," not as a scorecard against one brand's targets.

Point-in-time caveat: mention volume for every tracked entity is heavily front-loaded into the first two weeks of the window, tapering to near-zero for most entities after ~19 June with occasional isolated spikes. This most likely reflects tracking/collection cadence rather than a genuine decline in real-world online activity, and is noted where relevant rather than treated as a "declining interest" finding.

The two most obvious "official" brand names are nearly absent
  • Narrative ownership — Pillar 1: Third-party review sites, OTAs and travel agents are the fastest-growing named entities in the tracked set — Lonely Planet (+1,000% mentions), Trailfinders (+300%), MRT-related content (+450%), Tripadvisor (+85%) and Virgin Atlantic (+53%) — while Singapore's own official channel, visitsingapore.com, grew more modestly (+50% on a small base of 9) and Singapore Zoo mentions fell 31%.
  • Narrative ownership — Pillar 1: visitsingapore.com's strong #3 overall citation rank is almost entirely a single-model phenomenon: ChatGPT cites it in 70% of responses, but Deepseek and Perplexity cite it in 0% of theirs, and Google AI Mode/Overviews cite it in under 20%. On three of five models, the official site is effectively invisible.
  • Narrative ownership — Pillar 1: Google's AI Mode and AI Overviews source from a visibly different ecosystem than the other three models — Virgin Atlantic, Tripadvisor, Trailfinders, House of Travel, YouTube and MRT-operator content all score 0 on Deepseek, GPT-5 and Perplexity/Sonar in the heatmap, yet score 7–50 on Google's two AI surfaces. Google's AI answers for Singapore travel queries currently run through a commercial travel-intermediary ecosystem that the other three models don't draw on at all.
  • Narrative ownership — Pillar 1: Singapore Airlines is named zero times across all 125 responses, while a foreign carrier, Virgin Atlantic, is one of the fastest-growing named organisations in the set (11 mentions, +53%). The Singapore Tourism Board itself is named as an organisation in just 1 of 125 responses — its digital property performs well, but the institution's own name barely appears.
  • Perception — Pillar 2: Independent of any brand, AI models describe Singapore in uniformly positive terms — hawker food culture (40 key-phrase mentions), iconic attractions (Universal Studios Singapore named 59 times, Night Safari 42, S.E.A. Aquarium 33), and a "vibrant, multicultural city-state" narrative — with zero negative sentiment recorded across all 125 responses.
  • Brand-confusion flag: visitsingapore.org — a similarly-named but unrelated third-party domain (registered to a "Travelindex network" travel-directory operator, not the Singapore Tourism Board) — is independently cited 5 times across the tracked set, a small but real source of potential visitor confusion with the genuine visitsingapore.com.
Why this matters: AI models already tell a consistently positive, attraction-rich story about Singapore with no negative sentiment to overcome — the destination narrative itself needs no repair. The gap is entirely about ownership and routing: official channels and the national carrier are structurally under-named relative to review sites, OTAs and a foreign airline, and that gap is concentrated on specific models (Deepseek, Perplexity, Google's AI surfaces) rather than spread evenly.

Prompt-Level Deep Dive
Both tracked prompts and how each model responds — click to expand

"best places to visit in singapore" 58 responses — the attraction-listing prompt

▼
Deepseek

Produces a near-identical structured list across responses — Marina Bay Sands & SkyPark, Gardens by the Bay, Sentosa Island, Chinatown/Little India/Kampong Glam, Singapore Zoo & Night Safari, Clarke Quay, Botanic Gardens, Jewel Changi Airport — reasoning through the list before answering, citing no sources.

ChatGPT

Answers in a similar attraction-canon style but more often names visitsingapore.com or a specific attraction operator's own site as a supporting citation, and occasionally works in practical logistics (Singapore Tourist Pass, EZ-Link card).

Takeaway: This prompt behaves like a stable, largely citation-optional "top 10" answer — the canon of attractions is settled across models; the opportunity is less about winning a new mention and more about which URL gets attached to each attraction already named.

"singapore for holiday" 67 responses — the higher-intent, more commercially contested prompt

▼
Google AI Mode

Frequently surfaces booking-oriented results — flight options (Virgin Atlantic, Qantas), OTA package listings (Expedia, Trip.com, Klook), and Tripadvisor review snippets — reflecting the higher commercial intent of "for holiday" phrasing versus the more informational "best places to visit."

Perplexity

Leans on Reddit threads and independent blog roundups (neverendingfootsteps.com-style content) for practical "is it worth it / how many days" framing, rarely naming an official source.

Why this prompt is more contested: "For holiday" reads as closer to purchase intent than "best places to visit" — this is where OTAs, airlines and travel agents concentrate their presence (12 of 12 organisation-category "OTA/Agent/Airline" first-mentions in the dataset came from this prompt), making it the more commercially important of the two to win.

Competitive Gaps & Opportunities
Strategic fixes to close the ownership gap on a narrative that is already positive
Why this matters: Singapore does not need to fix how it's perceived — sentiment is uniformly positive with zero negative responses recorded. The opportunity is entirely about which brands and sites get credit for, and traffic from, a conversation that already favours the destination.
Gap IdentifiedImpactRecommended ActionBusiness Impact
visitsingapore.com's citation strength depends on one model Critical 70% of the domain's citations come from ChatGPT alone; Deepseek and Perplexity cite it 0% of the time, and Google's two AI surfaces under 20%. Fix: add Organization/TouristAttraction/FAQ schema and ensure content freshness signals that citation-capable models beyond ChatGPT (Perplexity/Sonar in particular, since it already cites other sources 70% of the time) have a concrete reason to link the official domain instead of Reddit or third-party blogs. The official site's aggregate #3 rank overstates its real reach — most AI-driven travellers on Deepseek or Perplexity never see it at all.
Singapore Airlines and the Singapore Tourism Board are nearly absent by name Critical Singapore Airlines: 0 of 125 responses. Singapore Tourism Board: 1 of 125. Meanwhile Virgin Atlantic is a top-5 fastest-growing tracked entity. Fix: assess whether this reflects genuinely thin owned/earned content mentioning these two institutions by name for holiday-planning queries, and pursue placements (official guides, partner content, structured airline/booking data) that name them directly rather than only their digital properties. The national carrier and the destination's own government body are structurally invisible in exactly the query type meant to route visitors toward them.
Google AI Mode / AI Overviews run on a different source ecosystem High Virgin Atlantic, Tripadvisor, Trailfinders, House of Travel, YouTube and MRT-operator content score 0 on Deepseek, GPT-5 and Sonar but 7–50 on Google's two AI surfaces. Fix: treat Google AI Mode/Overviews as a distinct optimisation target — these surfaces reward the commercial travel-booking ecosystem (OTA listings, airline schedules, review aggregators), not general web authority. A single "be more visible in AI" strategy will not work identically across all five models; Google's AI surfaces need their own content and partnership approach.
visitsingapore.org creates brand-confusion risk Medium An unrelated third-party domain with a near-identical name is independently cited 5 times. Fix: monitor this domain's growth and consider a clarifying note or outreach if citations increase, since AI models and users alike could conflate it with the genuine site. Small today, but domain confusion compounds as more AI-driven traffic starts from a citation rather than a typed URL.
"Singapore for holiday" is the more commercially contested prompt, and OTAs are winning it Medium All 12 first-mention wins classified as "OTA/Agent/Airline" in the dataset came from the higher-intent "for holiday" prompt, not the informational "best places to visit" prompt. Fix: prioritise official/attraction content specifically for higher-intent, booking-adjacent query phrasing, where third parties currently have the advantage. This is the prompt closest to an actual booking decision — it's also the one where official channels are weakest.
